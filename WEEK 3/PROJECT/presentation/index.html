How the two agents work together

Hermes is the intelligence engine running in the background. Icarus is the assistant you talk to via Telegram. They communicate through a simple API — Icarus asks, Hermes answers.

Hermes — the market watcher
Runs 24/7 on Railway (a cloud server). Five crawlers collect data on a schedule: news feeds every 6 hours, SEC filings daily, company news weekly, job postings on Wednesdays, earnings call transcripts on Thursdays. Claude AI reads every item and decides if it's relevant — then stores it.
Think of it as a researcher that never sleeps and never misses a filing.
Icarus — the assistant you talk to
Your personal Telegram bot, also powered by Claude. When you ask something like "What's happening with TSMC?" or "What macro themes are emerging this week?", Icarus picks the right Hermes tool, calls it, and gives you a clean answer — no searching required.
The interface between you and everything Hermes knows.
Redis — the memory store
Every signal Hermes finds is stored here instantly. Company profiles accumulate over time (permanent storage). Signals expire after 7 days so the database stays lean. The weekly digest and cluster snapshots also live here.
Fast key-value database — like a very organised filing cabinet in the cloud.
Upstash Vector — the search index
Every signal is also saved into a vector database — a special index that understands meaning, not just keywords. This is what makes "chip export controls" return signals from ASML, NVIDIA, and KLA even though you didn't name any of them.
Semantic search: finds what you mean, not just what you typed.
Rule 1: Hermes never pushes data anywhere. Icarus always pulls when asked. Clean separation of concerns.
Rule 2: Every new feature is wrapped in error handling. If the weekly digest fails, signals still store. Nothing cascades.
Rule 3: Every signal writes to Redis, the vector index, and the company profile in one atomic call — three databases, one operation.

Knowledge base & Claude AI integration

Two layers of context are injected into every prompt. Two Claude models handle different jobs. Five reusable templates keep the output consistent.

Primary knowledge base — who you are
Four Markdown documents Claude reads before writing anything: brand_voice.md (tone rules, forbidden phrases, post format), background.md (10yr procurement career), linkedin_examples.md (2 real posts with annotations), methodology.md (what makes a post worth writing).
Without this, Claude writes generic content. With it, drafts sound like you actually wrote them.
Secondary knowledge base — what's happening now
Two documents with live market context: hermes_signals_sample.md (real signals from NVIDIA, TSMC, Anthropic with procurement angles) and supplier_landscape.md (590 companies, 17 categories, tier rankings, content angles per category).
This is what makes drafts about real events — not training data from months ago.
Claude Haiku — the fast worker
Runs on every single crawl cycle. Reads each news item and decides: what type of signal is this? (funding, supply chain, regulatory, earnings, etc.) How urgent is it — HIGH, MEDIUM, or LOW? Is it significant enough to surface? Also handles profile enrichment summaries.
Cheap and fast — handles the volume. Processes thousands of items per month for ~$1–2.
Claude Sonnet — the deep thinker
Used for tasks that need real reasoning: writing LinkedIn post drafts, generating macro theme clusters (grouping signals across companies into themes), and producing the weekly market digest every Sunday.
More powerful and more expensive — reserved for output you actually read and act on.
LINKEDIN_POST: injects both KBs + signal, enforces brand voice, outputs 150–300 words
MARKET_BRIEF: condenses a list of signals into a procurement intelligence brief under 200 words
3 more templates: SIGNAL_ANALYSIS · CONTENT_CALENDAR · UNIQUENESS_COMPARISON — all reusable across any company or topic

What Hermes can actually do

Five specialised crawlers, each with a different job. Everything they collect feeds the same pipeline — classify, store, make queryable.

RSS feeds
Runs every 6 hours — covers all 590 suppliers plus 18 industry news sources
Free and unlimited — no reason to restrict which companies get monitored here
SEC EDGAR
Pulls official 8-K, 10-Q, and 10-K filings daily — only public companies that have a CIK number
Direct from the US government's filing database — no middleman, no paywalls
Tavily news
Weekly Monday search for company-specific news — Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers only (~177 companies)
Costs API credits, so Tier 3 is skipped — the tier system exists to protect this budget
Job postings
Every Wednesday — searches Lever, Greenhouse, and Workday for Tier 1+2 companies
Hiring spikes are 6-month leading indicators — a company aggressively hiring AI engineers tells you something before their press release does
Earnings transcripts
Every Thursday — fetches the full text of earnings call filings from SEC EDGAR (8-K type)
Dense forward-looking language ("we expect", "we're cautious about") that Claude classifies for supply chain and pricing signals
Signal detection — every item classified
Claude Haiku reads each item and assigns a signal type (supply chain, funding, pricing, regulatory...), an urgency (HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW), and a significance flag. Only flagged items surface to you.
Company profiles — living dossiers
Every signal permanently updates that company's profile: history, urgency breakdown, risk flags. At 10+ signals, Haiku auto-extracts key products and a risk summary.
"What do we know about Cerebras?" → full accumulated history, not just last 7 days.
Macro clustering + weekly digest
Sonnet groups signals into macro themes across all companies. Every Sunday 18:00 it writes a full category-by-category market summary — auto-delivered to Telegram at 18:30.
Cross-company patterns you'd never spot by querying one supplier at a time.
Semantic search + trend memory
Ask "chip export controls" — get results from ASML, NVIDIA, KLA without naming them. Trend delta compares this week's themes to last week: NEW / CONTINUING / RESOLVED.

Where data lives — Redis vs Vector

Every signal writes to two databases at once. Redis is fast structured storage for exact lookups. Upstash Vector is a search index that understands meaning. After 7 days the raw item is deleted — the intelligence extracted from it is not.

Redis — structured key-value storage
Signal item — what gets deleted after 7 days
KEY: hermes:item:rss_nvidia_7f3a2b { "supplier": "NVIDIA", "signal_type": "SUPPLY_CHAIN", "urgency": "HIGH", "title": "NVIDIA limits H100 exports to...", "significant": true } ⏱ TTL: 7 days → auto-deleted by Redis
Company profile — built from signals, stays forever
KEY: hermes:profile:nvidia { "signal_count": 47, "high_urgency": 12, "top_types": ["SUPPLY_CHAIN", "REGULATORY"], "risk_flags": ["export controls", "chip sanctions"], "risk_summary": "High US export policy exposure..." } ⏱ No TTL — permanent, updated with every new signal
Upstash Vector — semantic search index
Same signal — stored as meaning, not text
TEXT: "NVIDIA limits H100 exports due to new US export control regulations..." ↓ AI model converts text to numbers VECTOR: [0.021, -0.134, 0.089, 0.201...] (1024 numbers) META: { company: "NVIDIA", type: "SUPPLY_CHAIN", urgency: "HIGH" } ⏱ No TTL — stays even after the Redis item expires
What happens when you search
Query: "chip export controls" ↓ also converted to 1024 numbers Finds: nearest vectors by mathematical similarity Results: NVIDIA · ASML · KLA · Applied Materials → none of these companies were typed in the query → matched by meaning, not by keyword
After 7 days:  Raw signal deleted from Redis — the full text and URL are gone to keep storage lean.  |  Company profile remains — signal count, urgency history, risk flags, key products.  |  Vector entry remains — still searchable by meaning long after the item expires.
